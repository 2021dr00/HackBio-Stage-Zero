**The AI Doctor Will See You Now: How Genomics is Powering Personalised Medicine**

**Dharshana Rajkumar and Esan Ifeoluwa**

**Introduction**

Modern medicine has traditionally followed a one-size-fits-all approach, yet clinical outcomes vary widely, treatments effective for some patients may be ineffective or even harmful for others. This highlights a key limitation of conventional healthcare models.

Advances in genomics now enable the decoding of individual genetic variation, offering insights into disease risk and drug response, while artificial intelligence (AI) can analyse complex biological data to uncover patterns beyond human capability.

This article explores how the integration of AI and genomics is transforming healthcare from improving disease diagnosis to enabling targeted therapies, while also examining the key challenges limiting its widespread clinical adoption.

**Insights from recent research**

The integration of artificial intelligence (AI) with genomics is enhancing clinical decision-making by improving the accuracy of genomic analysis. Deep learning–based variant calling and functional prediction enable reliable interpretation of complex next-generation sequencing (NGS) data (Zubair, 2025).

AI also improves disease diagnosis, particularly in cancer genomics by integrating genomic, imaging, and clinical data for earlier detection and more precise classification, reflecting a shift toward multimodal diagnostics (Wang, 2024; Li, 2026).

Additionally, multi-omics approaches allow AI to combine genomics with other molecular layers, improving prognosis prediction and therapeutic target identification (Zhang, 2025). Together, these advances are driving precision oncology, where AI predicts drug response and guides personalised therapies based on individual molecular profiles.

**Practical Applications**

Genomics has made its mark in the advancement of clinical care. Through the detection of inherited abnormalities in patient genetic data, as well as data obtained from genome-wide association studies, its clinical relevance continues to expand. According to Chau & Choy (2021), the early detection of genetic disorders in the fetus has been improved by exome sequencing. In addition to that, analysing variants caused by deletions in sequencing and karyotyping studies has improved prenatal diagnosis (Zhang,2022).

From the identification of variants involved in genes associated with cardiovascular diseases, to the application of CRISPR-Cas9 in implementing more precise genome models in treatment plans, genomics is reshaping modern medicine. It also supports the development of less aggressive gene therapy approaches, particularly in cancer research, where targeted interventions are becoming increasingly viable.

**Challenges**

The high cost to implement and manage large datasets, alongside the lack or insufficient funding in research and development of these futuristic approaches, is a major barrier. There is also a constant need for training, which is labour-intensive and may not be practical in low- to middle-income countries. In addition, the need for structured policies on use and ethics within healthcare systems, to uphold data integrity, remains a significant challenge (Klitzman, 2011)

**Future Directions**

The advent of the Human Genome Project has led to the emergence of technologies that are less labour- and capital-intensive (Collins & Fink, 1995) . More recently, the integration of Artificial Intelligence in multi-omic studies promises to ease the challenges faced in genome sequencing and improve outcomes (Prokop, 2018), enabling faster data processing, improved accuracy in variant detection, and more reliable clinical decision-making processes.

**Conclusion**

The integration of AI and genomics is advancing the shift toward precise, patient-centred healthcare by enabling earlier diagnosis, accurate risk prediction, and targeted therapies. Despite ongoing challenges in cost, infrastructure, and data governance, continued progress in these fields holds the potential to improve clinical outcomes and expand access to personalized medicine ensuring more effective and tailored treatment for patients.
